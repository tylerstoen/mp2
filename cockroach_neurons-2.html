Effect of Citronellal Odor Puffs on Cockroach Neurons 1, 2, and 3

Goal

We compare the effect of citronellal odor puff stimulation on the firing rate of two cockroach antennal lobe neurons (Neuron 1, Neuron 2 and Neuron 3).

We will:

  1. Visualize spike trains.
  2. Form a qualitative hypothesis.
  3. Fit one NHPP model per neuron.
  4. Visualize stimulus effects.
  5. Determine whether the effect differs across neurons.

Raster Plots: Neuron 1

Based on the raster plots for Neuron 1, stimulation seems to lead to higher firing rate and dense spikes. Model Specification

We approximate the non-homogeneous Poisson process by binning time.

Assume:

\[ Y_{it} \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_i(t)\Delta t) \]

Using the log link:

\[ \log(\lambda_i(t)) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{stim}(t) + f(t) + b_{\text{trial}} \]

Where:

  • \(\beta_1\) = stimulation effect (log scale)
  • \(f(t)\) = smooth baseline firing pattern
  • \(b_{\text{trial}} \sim N(0,\sigma^2)\) = trial random effect
  • \(\log(\Delta t)\) = offset

Fit Model: Neuron 1

df_n1$trial <- factor(df_n1$trial)

fit_n1 <- gam(
  y ~ stim + s(t, bs = "cr", k = 15) + s(trial, bs = "re"),
  family = poisson(),
  offset = log_dt,
  gamma = 1.4,
  data = df_n1,
  method = "REML"
)

Stimulus Effect on Intensity: Neuron 1

  • The plot shows the estimated smooth time effect from the fitted GAM for neuron 1.
  • Specifically, we plot \(\exp(\hat{f}(t))\), the exponentiated smooth function of time.
  • \(f(t)\) captures time-varying structure in the log firing rate not explained by the stimulus term.
  • Exponentiating converts the smooth from the log scale to a multiplicative scale for firing intensity.
  • The black curve shows the estimated effect \(\exp(\hat{f}(t))\).
  • The dashed bands represent an approximate 95% confidence interval.
  • The red shaded region marks the stimulus window (odor puff).
  • The horizontal line at 1 indicates the reference level of the smooth (no multiplicative change).
p1 <- plot(fit_n1, select = 1)[[1]]
x1 <- p1$x
y1 <- exp(p1$fit)
lo1 <- exp(p1$fit - 2 * p1$se)
hi1 <- exp(p1$fit + 2 * p1$se)

plot(x1, y1, type = "n",
     xlab = "t (s)",
     ylab = "multiplicative effect",
     main = "Neuron 1: Effect of Stimulation")

polygon(c(x1, rev(x1)), c(lo1, rev(hi1)),
        border = NA,
        col = adjustcolor("gray", 0.25))

usr <- par("usr")
rect(5.99, usr[3], 6.49, usr[4],
     border = NA,
     col = adjustcolor("red", 0.20))

lines(x1, y1, lwd = 2)
abline(h = 1, lty = 2)

Interpretation:

  • If curve > 1 → increased firing
  • If curve < 1 → suppression
  • If CI includes 1 → weak/no evidence

Because the curve and confidence bands lie entirely above 1 during and immediately following the stimulus, we have evidence that the stimulus leads to increased firing in neuron 1.

Final Comparison

1. Do citronellal odor puffs affect firing rate?

Yes. The models show that the multiplicative effect of the stimulus differs from 1 for all neurons.

  • Neuron 1: The multiplicative effect is greater than 1 during and shortly after the stimulus window. During this period, the confidence band lies above 1, indicating that the citronellal odor puff increases firing rate of this neuron.

  • Neuron 2: The multiplicative effect rises above 1 during the stimulus window, indicating that the citronellal odor puff increases firing rate while the stimulus is active. The confidence band does not include 1, so the increase is further supported. Immediately after the stimulus, both the multiplicative effect and confidence band drop below 1, indicating a decrease in firing rate following the stimulus.

  • Neuron 3: The multiplicative effect is below 1duringthe stimulus window, indicating that the citronellal odor puff suppressed the neuron firing rate while it was in effect. Immediately after the stimulus, however, the effect risesabove 1, suggesting a delayed increase in firing rate. The confidence band lies below 1 during the stimulus window, further providing evidence that the odor puff suppressed the firing rate.

2. Is the effect uniform across neurons?

Possible outcomes:

  • Both increase → uniform excitatory effect

  • One increases, one decreases → neuron-specific response

  • One significant, one null → heterogeneous sensitivity

  • Both null → no detectable effect

No, the effect of citronellal odor stimulation is not uniform across the neurons.

The direction and magnitude of the response differ across the three neurons.

  • Neuron 1 shows a clear increase in firing rate during the stimulus window, and the confidence band lies above 1, indicating strong evidence of an excitatory effect.

  • Neuron 2 also shows an increase during the stimulus, although the magnitude of the effect is smaller and the confidence band reflects greater uncertainty.

  • Neuron 3, in contrast, shows a decrease in firing rate during the stimulus window, with the multiplicative effect falling below 1 and the confidence band supporting the evidence of suppression.

Because two neurons show increased firing rate while one shows decreased firing rate, the response to the citronellal stimulation appears to be neuron-specific rather than uniform across neurons.

Conclusion

Based on the fitted NHPP models:

  • Neuron 2: increases during stimulus, decreases after

  • Neuron 3: decrease during stimulus, increase after

Therefore, citronellal odor puffs do affect firing rate, and the effect is not uniform across neurons.
